# 🛸 Why Andromeda Spins: A Layman's Guide

## The Problem: The "Missing" Gravity

Imagine you are at a playground, spinning a merry-go-round. If you spin it very fast, you have to hold on tight or you’ll be thrown off.

Galaxies like **Andromeda (M31)** are spinning so fast that, according to standard physics, they should fly apart. To explain why they stay together, scientists usually invent **Dark Matter**—an invisible "glue" that provides extra gravity.

## The CODE-GEO Solution: "Informational Friction"

In the **krylov-drag** project, we don't think there is any invisible glue. Instead, we look at how the universe processes information.

### 1. The Holographic Boost (The "Power Steering")

Think of space not as empty nothingness, but as a **Data Substrate**. When a galaxy is very large and the gravity is very weak (at the edges), the "Data Substrate" kicks in to help.

* It’s like **Power Steering** for gravity. It boosts the force just enough to keep the stars in orbit. This is what we call the **Entropic Boost**.

### 2. The Krylov Drag (The "Top Speed")

However, nothing is free. As the galaxy spins, it creates "complexity" in the data substrate. Eventually, the substrate can't keep up perfectly—it hits a **Complexity Plateau**.

* This creates a tiny bit of **Informational Friction**.
* This "Drag" is why we see the stars at the very edge of Andromeda slowing down just a little bit, as discovered in the **Zhang et al. (2024)** study.

## Summary: No Ghost Particles Needed

We don't need Dark Matter. The "extra gravity" is just a natural feature of how the universe's "processor" handles large, spinning objects.

By calculating the **Drag**, we can predict exactly how fast the galaxy spins using only the stars and gas we can actually see.
